# the openhouse early years programme — parent positioning

*Ages 3–5 · the foundation years.* Working doc for the parent-facing story, the parent pulse, and the eventual hub overview. Grounded in India's NCF Foundational Stage (NEP 2020), the EYFS (UK), Head Start ELOF, and early-childhood research.

---

## the one line

> **The years from 3 to 5 are when a child builds the foundations everything else stands on. Our early years programme grows the *whole* child — through art, through stem, and through language & storytelling — and lays the groundwork for the art & design, robotics, and public-speaking journeys that come at 5+.**

## why all three, together

A young child doesn't develop in subjects — they develop as a whole person, all at once. Every recognised early-learning framework says the same thing in its own words: the early years are about a small set of **interlocking domains** — the body, feelings and getting along with others, thinking and problem-solving, language, and creativity — and the best way to grow them is **play**.

- India's **NCF Foundational Stage** sets out five domains — *physical · socio-emotional & ethical · cognitive · language & literacy · aesthetic & cultural* — plus the habits that make a good learner, and names **play as the core way children learn**.
- The UK's **EYFS** and the US **Head Start ELOF** organise the early years the same way, and both treat **spoken language and creative, hands-on exploration as foundations under everything else**.

Art, stem, and language aren't three separate add-ons — they're **three doors into the same whole-child development**. A storytelling session grows language *and* confidence *and* sequencing. A building session grows problem-solving *and* fine motor control *and* persistence. Run together, they cover the whole child; run as the foundation, they feed directly into what comes next.

## what each strand gives your child

| strand | what they learn (3–5) | why it matters | what it builds toward (5+) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **art & design** | exploring real materials and tools; making deliberate choices of colour, shape and composition; expressing an idea or feeling; looking closely | builds fine-motor control, imagination, self-expression and the confidence to make and share — and the habit of *noticing* | the **art & design** programme — observation, technique, and a personal visual voice |
| **stem** | building and testing; sorting, patterns and early numbers; asking "what happens if…"; figuring things out by trying | grows logical thinking, problem-solving, hand-eye coordination, and persistence — children as young as four can already think like little engineers | the **robotics** programme — engineering thinking, sequencing/coding logic, applied maths |
| **language & storytelling** | listening to and re-telling stories; new words; describing pictures; telling a story with a beginning, middle and end; speaking to the group | oral storytelling is one of the strongest ways to build language — and strong early narrative predicts later reading, writing *and* speaking | the **public speaking** programme — communication, narrative, audience awareness |

## the ladder — foundation now, mastery later

```
   ages 3–5 (foundation)            ages 5+ (mastery)
   ─────────────────────            ─────────────────
   art & design strand   ─────────▶  art & design
   stem strand           ─────────▶  robotics
   language strand       ─────────▶  public speaking
        (music & fitness strand  ─▶  music & dance — coming)
```

Nothing in the 5+ programmes appears from nowhere. The child who *made and explained* an artwork at four steps into art & design ready to look and create with intent. The child who *built and tested* steps into robotics already thinking in steps and cause-and-effect. The child who *told stories to the circle* steps into public speaking already knowing a story has bones and an audience has eyes.

## and it readies them for school

Beyond our own ladder, the same foundations are exactly what India's NEP/NCF Foundational Stage calls **school readiness** — not early academics, but the **positive learning habits** that make formal school work: focusing, persisting, getting along, regulating big feelings, and a love of finding things out. Our daily frame builds these on purpose: a circle time that settles and focuses, a closing reflection that names feelings and recaps learning, and a educator who *observes and notices* every child rather than testing them.

## ready-to-use parent lines (short form)

- **the whole programme:** "At 3–5 your child isn't learning subjects — they're building the foundations everything stands on. We grow the whole child through art, stem, and storytelling, and set them up for art & design, robotics and public speaking later."
- **art:** "Making art at this age builds the hands, the imagination, and the confidence to express an idea — the start of the art & design journey."
- **stem:** "Building and testing teaches your child to think in steps and solve problems — children this age can already think like little engineers. It's where robotics begins."
- **language:** "Telling and re-telling stories is the single best way to grow language — and it's the seed of confident public speaking."
- **why no tests:** "We don't test 3-year-olds. Educators *observe* and record what your child can actually do, in a book that comes home each term."
