# imagine playground — the activities (final, by level)

*The STEM strand's building block. One integrated set of 11 projects that run in order across the year — children build with tracks, action bricks and ramps and, through them, grow four STEM skills. This is the educator's source of truth: what each activity is, how to run it, and how the **same activity** stretches across **level 1 (easy) → level 2 (medium) → level 3 (hard)**.*

> **levels are by attainment, not age.** Every activity runs at all three levels. The educator sets the level to match the child — a child can be level 3 on counting and level 1 on patterns on the same day. Easy/medium/hard differ only in the **independence, detail and reasoning** expected; the activity is the same.

---

## the four skills (and what grows inside each)

Every imagine playground activity builds these four skills. Each skill has four **abilities** that deepen across levels.

| skill | ability 1 | ability 2 | ability 3 | ability 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **curiosity** | notices | wonders | predicts | investigates |
| **problem solving** | recognises | tries | persists | solves |
| **logic** | sorts / classifies | patterns / sequences | predicts from rule | reasons |
| **number sense** | connects quantity | relates numbers | operates with quantities | measures / compares |

### the learning outcomes — easy · medium · hard

**curiosity** — *spiral, not linear; children notice, wonder, predict and investigate across every activity.*

| ability | level 1 · easy | level 2 · medium | level 3 · hard |
|---|---|---|---|
| notices | notices obvious features/changes — colour, size, sound, movement, a train stopping | notices changes before/after testing or when revisiting the same material | notices smaller details, repeated changes, or differences across multiple trials |
| wonders | asks "what" questions or responds to "what do you see?" | asks why/how questions about familiar events | asks explanation-seeking questions and follows up after a result |
| predicts | guesses when prompted: "will it move?" "near or far?" | predicts before testing and gives a simple reason | predicts using prior experience, visible evidence, or a rule |
| investigates | tries an action to see what happens, with support | changes one action/material and observes the result | tests an idea deliberately, compares prediction with result, explains what was found |

**problem solving** — *children meet small play problems: broken tracks, wrong signals, ramps that don't reach, loads that don't match, chains that stop.*

| ability | level 1 · easy | level 2 · medium | level 3 · hard |
|---|---|---|---|
| recognises | notices something is not working | identifies where/what the problem is | names the specific problem without being told |
| tries | attempts one action with support | tries a different approach when the first fails | tries two+ strategies and selects one based on result |
| persists | tries again after encouragement | returns to the problem after one failed attempt | keeps testing and adjusting until the goal is reached |
| solves | fixes a simple problem with help | explains what changed to make it work | names the cause and explains the fix: "it stopped because… I changed…" |

**logic** — *the most syllabus-like: sorting/matching → patterning/sequencing → rule-based prediction → if-then → cause-effect.*

| ability | level 1 · easy | level 2 · medium | level 3 · hard |
|---|---|---|---|
| sorts / classifies | sorts by one visible feature (colour, shape, size, type) | sorts by two features, or re-sorts by a new rule | creates own sorting rule and explains why items belong/don't |
| patterns / sequences | continues AB patterns; first-next-last | creates AB/AAB/ABB patterns; orders 3–5 steps | creates complex movement/signal/route patterns and names the rule |
| predicts from rule | matches same colour/symbol/action | uses a simple if-then rule | uses two-condition rules or route constraints |
| reasons | says what happened | explains with "because" | explains multi-step cause-effect or rule-based outcome |

**number sense** — *the most linear skill. Differentiate through easy/medium/hard — don't expect all 3–5s to reach the same number outcomes.*

| ability | level 1 · easy | level 2 · medium | level 3 · hard |
|---|---|---|---|
| connects quantity | counts to 10; recognises small quantities; matches number to quantity | counts to 20/30; records quantities with drawings/objects | counts sets to 50; writes numbers to 50; rote counts to 100 (extension) |
| relates numbers | compares more/less/same; before/after in routines | before/after/between within 20/30; orders numbers | counts forward/backward within 50; compares two-digit numbers concretely |
| operates with quantities | adds/removes informally in play | join/take-away with objects within 15/20 | counting-on/removing strategies; word problems within 20 |
| measures / compares | big/small, long/short, near/far, full/empty, heavy/light | measures with non-standard units and compares | records, orders and uses measurement/data to solve problems |

> **number-sense caution:** advanced outcomes (counting to 100, tens/ones to 50, odd/even, skip counting, word problems within 20) stay as **hard extensions for ready children** — never an expectation for every 3–5-year-old.

## the knowledge children build

Across the strand children pick up practical STEM knowledge in five domains:

- **people & shared spaces** — people travel, wait, drive, guide, follow rules, share spaces safely.
- **places, routes & paths** — tracks, roads, stations, destinations, bridges, tunnels, ramps, loops and branching paths connect places.
- **vehicles & loads** — trains move on tracks; engines pull cars; vehicles carry passengers or freight; loads can be full/empty, heavy/light, more/less.
- **safety, signals & rules** — signs, lights, sounds, colours, tickets and rules help people know what to do and move safely.
- **motion** — things move, stop, turn, roll, slide, fall, go down ramps, travel near/far, and move around/through/over/under.

## the 11 activities, in order

> Each activity follows the same shape: **engage** (whole group) → **explore** (groups, where the levels live) → **explain** (debrief — this is where the learning is checked) → **elaborate** (stretch). The **easy/medium/hard** options sit inside explore and elaborate. The **learning check** at the end tells you if a child has the skill.

---

### 1 · train time
*getting started — children begin counting as they build tracks and start to explore sequence and order.*

- **skills** — curiosity: notices what happens when track pieces are joined, removed or turned · problem solving: recognises when the train can't move and tries to fix the track · logic: reasons that a connected path is needed for movement · number sense: counts pieces, compares track length, combines groups.
- **knowledge** — a train moves on a track; track pieces join to make a path; the engine pulls the cars; a broken / missing / wrongly-turned piece stops movement.
- **materials** — track pieces · train engine · train cars · *optional:* station & destination markers.
- **grouping** — educator explains to all 10 together; children work in 2–3 groups.

**A · engage.** educator: "today we are going to build a train track." Ask: "what does a train do? where does it move? what sound does it make?" Show one track piece: "this is a track piece — pieces join to make a path for the train." Join two. Ask: "what happened when I joined them? can the train move on this?" Show engine + car: "the engine pulls the train cars." Place the train and move it. Ask: "why could the train move? what if the track is not joined?"

**B · explore.** Each group builds one track. Ask: "how many pieces have you used? which piece next? is your track getting longer? where will the train start? where will it go?" Children count each group's pieces. Ask: "which group used more / fewer? what if we join both tracks?" Children join tracks and move trains.
- **easy** — build with 4–6 pieces, count aloud, use long/short and more/less.
- **medium** — build two tracks, count each, compare more/fewer, join and count the total.
- **hard** — build with an exact number of pieces (e.g. 14 or 18); the track must reach its destination and pass a landmark.

**C · explain.** Ask: "what does a train need to move? what happened when the track was joined? what if one piece was missing?" Remove a piece. Ask: "what is the problem? where did the train stop? what can we try?" Children fix and test. Turn one piece the wrong way. Ask: "why did the train stop? which piece caused it? what did you change? how do you know it's fixed?"

**D · elaborate.** easy: build a track from one child to another · medium: build from station to picnic area and count the pieces · hard: build from station to picnic area using exactly 14 pieces.

**learning check** — child can count pieces, compare tracks, recognise a broken path, fix it, and explain: *"the train stopped because…"*

### 2 · first trip
*children learn simple train movement and how action bricks help complete a journey.*

- **skills** — curiosity: notices how signals change movement · problem solving: figures out how to stop/restart the train · logic: sequences start–stop–destination · number sense: uses first-next-last and counts stops.
- **knowledge** — people travel from one place to another; journeys have starts and destinations; signals guide movement.
- **materials** — train · track pieces · red & green blocks · station/destination materials · *optional:* stop signs.
- **grouping** — line up like a train (hands gently on the shoulders ahead); educator holds a green flag for "go" and red for "stop"; play the train game. Then build in 2–3 groups.

**A · engage.** Ask: "have you taken a train, metro, bus or tram? where did you start? where did you reach?" Movement game: green = move, red = stop. Ask: "what made us stop? what made us move again?"

**B · explore.** Children build a double-ended track. educator: "one end is the station, the other is the destination." Children choose a destination — park, zoo, picnic area, home. Move the train station→destination; test stopping with the red block and restarting with green.
- **easy** — one station, one destination, one stop signal.
- **medium** — add one stop between station and destination, and change the signal before the train arrives.
- **hard** — plan and map a route with two stops before the destination; signals must be placed in the correct order.

**C · explain.** Ask: "where did the train start? where did it stop? where did it reach? what did the red signal do? the green signal?"

**D · elaborate.** easy: move station→destination, stop once · medium: station → stop 1 → destination · hard: design a trip with a station, two stops, destination and return.

**learning check** — child can describe the route order and explain what each signal does.

### 3 · load the train
*children connect numbers to quantities by loading the right amount of freight.*

- **skills** — curiosity: notices how many objects fit · problem solving: corrects too many/too few · logic: sorts/classifies freight · number sense: counts, compares, composes/decomposes, adds/removes.
- **knowledge** — vehicles carry people or things; freight = things carried; cars can be full/empty/heavy/light; different freight may go to different places.
- **materials** — train cars · number cards · small objects (blocks, crayons, erasers, toy food/tools) · sorting mats · destination cards (market, school, repair shop, picnic area).
- **grouping** — educator explains to all; children work in 2 groups.

**A · engage.** educator: "today we're loading a freight train — freight means things a train carries." Show mixed objects. Ask: "what do you see? which are the same? which different? what could go together?" Sort a few by type: "my rule is — food things together." Ask: "what other rule could we use?"

**B · explore.** Children **sort freight first.** Ask: "what's your sorting rule? colour, size, type or use? which object doesn't belong?" Then **load**: put number 3 on a car — "this car has number 3, we load 3 things." Children choose a number, count objects, load, check.
- **easy** — sort by one feature; match numbers 1–10 to quantities.
- **medium** — sort by two features; numbers to 20; compare more/less/equal; show 8 as 5+3 and 6+2.
- **hard** — solve freight stories within 20: *"the picnic area needs 15 things, it has 9 — how many more?" "the train has 16 tools, deliver 7 — how many remain?"*

**C · explain.** Ask: "what was your sorting rule? why do these belong together? what number is on the train? how many did you load? does the number match? which car has more/less? how did you know how many more to add?"

**D · elaborate.** easy: sort one group and load the correct quantity · medium: sort two groups and load different quantities · hard: create a delivery problem with a quantity and a sorting rule — *"only blue blocks and small food can go to the picnic area; choose and load 12."*

**learning check** — child can sort, count, match number to quantity, compare, and solve add/remove load problems.

### 4 · longest track
*children compare track lengths and begin measuring distance.*

- **skills** — curiosity: notices which track reaches farther · problem solving: changes the design to reach farther · logic: orders by length/distance · number sense: measures and compares.
- **knowledge** — tracks can be long/short, reach near/far places, and routes can be compared by distance.
- **materials** — track pieces · train cars · same-sized blocks · measuring tape · recording sheet · blocks for towers.
- **grouping** — educator demo with all; track building in 2 groups; tower building in pairs.

**A · engage.** Show a short and a long track. Ask: "which is longer? which shorter? how can we check?" Measure with same-sized blocks.

**B · explore.** Each group builds a track out from the wall. educator: "build a track that reaches as far as possible." Measure with blocks/tape; record the result.
- **easy** — compare long/short by looking.
- **medium** — measure with blocks and compare.
- **hard** — build the farthest-reaching track using a fixed number of pieces; record and compare.

**C · explain.** Ask: "which track reached farther? how did we check? did more curves help or not? what changed when you rebuilt?"

**D · elaborate.** Build towers. easy: tall/short · medium: measure with blocks · hard: build the tallest tower using exactly 10 blocks and make it stand.

**learning check** — child can compare, measure, record and revise a route/structure.

### 5 · train sound
*children use action bricks more purposefully to create a planned journey with signals.*

- **skills** — curiosity: notices signal effects · problem solving: places signals to help the train · logic: connects signal to action and creates patterns · number sense: counts signals and stops.
- **knowledge** — sounds, colours and lights can be signals; train sounds warn people; vehicles may need fuel/energy.
- **materials** — train · track pieces · yellow, blue, white, red, green blocks · tunnel · pieces for picnic area / fuel station.
- **grouping** — movement game with all; station building in groups; the train route as a whole group.

**A · engage.** Ask: "what sound does a train make? how do people know a train is coming?" Movement signals: yellow = choo-choo and move · blue = stop for fuel · red = stop · green = go. Create a pattern: yellow, blue, yellow, blue. Ask: "what comes next?"

**B · explore.** Children build a picnic area, fuel station, train and track. Move the train picnic→fuel. Test the coloured blocks, then place signal patterns: yellow-blue-yellow-blue · red-green-red-green · yellow-yellow-blue repeated.
- **easy** — use one signal→action connection; continue an AB pattern with support.
- **medium** — use 2–3 signals and create an AB/AAB/ABB pattern.
- **hard** — design a route using at least 4 signals in a repeating rule, and explain the rule.

**C · explain.** Ask: "what did yellow do? what did blue do? what was your pattern? what repeated? what comes next? why might a train need sound / fuel / light?"

**D · elaborate.** Add the tunnel and white block. easy: place white near the tunnel and observe · medium: create a signal pattern around the tunnel · hard: design a full route with sound, fuel, light, stop and go signals in a repeating rule.

**learning check** — child can connect signal to action, continue/create a pattern, count signals and explain their purpose.

### 6 · o-shaped track — looping
*children understand repeated journeys through a circular track.*

- **skills** — curiosity: notices the repeated route · problem solving: builds a closed loop and fixes a gap · logic: understands loop/repetition/route pattern · number sense: counts loops and repeated stops.
- **knowledge** — a loop goes around and comes back, allowing repeated journeys and repeated visits.
- **materials** — curved track pieces · train · coloured blocks · blocks for destinations.
- **grouping** — movement loop with all; destination building in groups.

**A · engage.** Ask: "what do you do again and again every day?" Body pattern: clap-jump-clap-jump. Then: clap-clap-jump, clap-clap-jump. Ask: "what is repeating?"

**B · explore.** Build an O-shaped track. Add destinations: station, park, fuel, castle. Move the train around. Create a route pattern: *station → park → station → park*, or *station → park → fuel → station → park → fuel*.
- **easy** — move around the loop once and notice the return.
- **medium** — repeat a two-stop route twice.
- **hard** — create a three-stop repeated route and explain the rule.

**C · explain.** Ask: "what's different about this track? which place did the train visit again? what repeated? what comes after park? how do you know?"

**D · elaborate.** Compare the O-shaped track with a double-ended track. easy: look and compare · medium: move trains on both and describe the difference · hard: choose the better track for repeated trips and explain why.

**learning check** — child can identify the loop, the repeated route, the pattern and repeated visits.

---

### 7 · y-shaped track — conditional statements
*children explore choices, routes, and simple "if-then" thinking.*

- **skills** — curiosity: wonders where the train will go · problem solving: chooses a route · logic: uses if-then rules and constraints · number sense: counts destinations/passengers.
- **knowledge** — branching paths create choices; tickets/signals can tell people where to go.
- **materials** — y-shaped track · train · coloured tickets/tokens · coloured stops · building pieces.
- **grouping** — ticket game with all; track building together.

**A · engage.** Create stops: red = playground · blue = park · green = zoo. Give out tickets. educator: "if you have a red ticket, then go to the playground." Ask: "what told you where to go?"

**B · explore.** Build a Y-shaped track with two stops. Children use tickets to decide the route.
- **easy** — match ticket colour to the same-colour stop.
- **medium** — use an if-then rule with two/three destinations.
- **hard** — use two conditions: *"if red ticket and freight, go left" · "if red ticket and passengers, go right."*

**C · explain.** Ask: "how many choices did the train have? what did the ticket tell us? if the ticket was blue, where did the train go? what was your rule? (hard) what two things did you check?"

**D · elaborate.** Add more destinations or rules. easy: two stops · medium: three stops · hard: children create their own route rule and explain it.

**learning check** — child can match, use if-then, count choices and explain a route rule.

### 8 · journey — trouble on the road
*children apply route planning and action bricks to solve traffic/safety problems.*

- **skills** — curiosity: notices signs and guesses their meaning · problem solving: identifies a route/safety problem and chooses a sign · logic: sorts signs by purpose and connects problem to rule · number sense: counts signs/stops where relevant.
- **knowledge** — roads/tracks are shared spaces; signs and rules help people move safely.
- **materials** — train · y-shaped track · traffic light/signs · coloured blocks · building cards · paper/crayons for signs.
- **grouping** — traffic discussion + movement game with all; building in groups.

**A · engage.** Ask: "what rules do we follow on roads? what does red mean? green?" Movement game: stop/go with signals. Show signs and sort them: stop/go signs · warning signs · direction signs. Ask: "what is the rule for this group?"

**B · explore.** Children build models and a Y-shaped track. educator places signs/blocks along the route; children drive the train and observe. Ask: "what happened here? was there a problem? what sign could help?"
- **easy** — identify red stop and green go.
- **medium** — match a problem to a sign and sort signs by purpose.
- **hard** — create a new sign for a specific road problem and place it correctly.

**C · explain.** Ask: "what problem did you see? which sign helped? why did we need it? what kind of sign is it? how did it keep people safe?"

**D · elaborate.** Children create safety signs. easy: draw a stop/go sign · medium: place a sign at the correct spot · hard: design signs for a problem and explain the rules.

**learning check** — child can identify a safety problem, sort signs, choose a sign and explain why the rule helps.

---

### 9 · ramps
*children explore motion, gravity, prediction, and distance through rolling objects.*

- **skills** — curiosity: predicts how far an object will go · problem solving: changes ramp/object to reach a target · logic: reasons that a change affects movement · number sense: measures/compares/records distance.
- **knowledge** — a ramp is a sloping path; objects can roll/slide down ramps; objects can travel near/far.
- **materials** — small & big ramps · cars/rolling objects · number markers · paper for recording · blocks/magna-tiles · objects: ball, block, car.
- **grouping** — intro with all; testing in 2 groups.

**A · engage.** Ask: "what happens on a slide? do you stay at the top or go down?" educator: "a ramp is like a slide for objects." Show a car, ball, block. Ask: "which will roll? which might slide? which will go far?"

**B · explore.** Set blocks numbered 1–10 after the ramp as distance markers. Use the small ramp — predict, roll, mark. Use the big ramp (two ramps joined) — predict, roll, mark. Test different objects.
- **easy** — predict near/far with one ramp.
- **medium** — compare small/big ramp and two objects.
- **hard** — make the car stop between two numbers without pushing it.

**C · explain.** Ask: "which ramp made it go farther? where did it stop? what changed? which object rolled? which slid? what can we change to reach the target?"

**D · elaborate.** easy: try a different object and say roll/slide · medium: test two objects and compare · hard: reach a target zone by changing ramp height/start/object.

**learning check** — child can predict, test, mark, compare, adjust and explain movement.

### 10 · chain reaction
*children extend cause-and-effect thinking by designing a sequence where one action triggers the next.*

- **skills** — curiosity: predicts what happens next · problem solving: finds where the chain stops and changes one part · logic: sequences first-next-last and reasons cause-effect · number sense: counts events in order.
- **knowledge** — objects can move, fall, push or trigger other objects.
- **materials** — inspiration photo · blocks · objects that fall/roll/push · open testing space.
- **grouping** — story with all; building in small groups of 3–4; the final chain as a whole group.

**A · engage.** Show a picture. Ask: "what might move first? what might happen next?" Tell a story: "a ride goes up. it is released. it drops. it pushes a switch. a flag goes up." Ask: "what happened first? next? last?"

**B · explore.** Groups build one action causing another from the inspiration photo (the educator helps assign the separate parts). Examples: falling block pushes block · rolling object hits tower · ramp sends car into block. Ask: "what will happen first? what will that make happen? what at the end?" Children test the reactions.
- **easy** — two-step reaction.
- **medium** — three-step reaction.
- **hard** — four or more steps, with repair after testing.

**C · explain.** Ask: "what happened first? next? last? where did it stop? why did it stop? what did you change?"

**D · elaborate.** Combine groups' reactions into one longer chain. easy: name first/last · medium: connect two reactions · hard: find the failure point, repair it and explain.

**learning check** — child can sequence events, predict, identify a failure, persist and explain cause/fix.

---

### 11 · probability
*children end with the most abstract idea: predicting, recording, and comparing chance outcomes.*

- **skills** — curiosity: predicts a possible colour · problem solving: builds with the colours received · logic: sorts results and understands "more likely / not sure" · number sense: counts, records, compares most/least.
- **knowledge** — some game results are uncertain; a spinner can land on different colours.
- **materials** — colour wheel/spinner · coloured blocks · recording sheet · sorting mats · crayons.
- **grouping** — intro with all; spins by turn; building in small groups.

**A · engage.** educator gives a colour clue: "it is the colour of a fire engine." Children guess. Then show three colours with no clue. Ask: "is this easier or harder?" educator: "sometimes we guess, but we don't know for sure."

**B · explore.** Show the spinner. Before each spin: "which colour do you think it will land on?" Spin and record the actual colour; place a matching block on the colour mat.
- **easy** — predict and identify the actual colour; sort by colour.
- **medium** — count outcomes and compare more/less/equal.
- **hard** — discuss which colour has a better chance if it has more spaces — but may not always come.

**C · explain.** Ask: "what did you predict? what actually came? how did we sort the results? which came most? least? if one colour has more spaces, what might happen? can we be completely sure?"

**D · elaborate.** After 5 spins, children build a prize using their collected colour blocks. easy: sort and name colours · medium: count more/less colours · hard: predict the most-collected colour, compare with actual and explain.

**learning check** — child can predict, sort, count, compare and understand uncertain outcomes.

## coverage at a glance (where each skill is strongest)

- **curiosity** — every activity (observation of change/result); prediction strongest in ramps, probability, chain reaction, train time.
- **problem solving** — designed failure moments in train time, ramps, chain reaction, trouble on the road. *Persistence depends on the educator allowing retries and not fixing too quickly.*
- **logic** — sorting in load the train / trouble on the road / probability; patterning in train sound / o-shaped track / first trip / chain reaction; if-then in y-shaped track / train sound / probability.
- **number sense** — counting & matching in load the train / train time / probability; more/less & ordering in load the train / longest track / ramps; measurement in longest track / ramps. *Operating with quantities is medium/hard only.*

> **the one rule that makes it work:** the **explain/debrief** is the assessment. If you skip it, the building was just play. The questions in each "explain" section are written to check exactly what the activity intended to teach — ask them, take a few answers, and you'll know each child's level.
