# levels & skills — which skills are *really* level-differentiated (and which aren't)

You were right: I wrongly labelled **every** skill's ability-ladder as "levels." That conflates two different things. This note fixes the model, grounded in research, and lists which 3–5 skills are which — **to confirm before we rebuild the LOs and the plans.**

---

## the mistake, named
- **what a level *should* mean:** for the *same* skill, a **genuinely different ladder of learning outcomes** *and* a **different milestone (north star)** at each level — because what the child is learning has actually changed.
- **what I did:** took **one** breakdown of a skill and just stuck "easy / medium / hard" labels on its rungs. That's *one* ladder relabelled — not real levels.

## the rule that tells them apart (research-backed)
There are two kinds of things we grow at 3–5, and they behave differently:

**① content / "learning-trajectory" skills → real levels.** Some skills have a *developmental progression where the goal itself changes* — the canonical examples are **early maths** (Clements & Sarama's *learning trajectories*: subitize → count → cardinality → add/take-away → tens & ones) and **phonological awareness** (the continuum: rhyme/syllable → onset-rime → phoneme). Here a "level" is a **different target with a different milestone** — two children can be doing genuinely different maths in the same game. These skills get **multiple ladders + a milestone per level.**

**② dispositions / continuous skills → one ladder.** Curiosity, creativity, persistence, confidence are **dispositions** — "how children learn," grown by *modelling*, **continuously**, not in discrete stages (EYLF; the dispositions-vs-skills literature). The *milestone is the same for everyone*; children just sit at different points and move at different speeds. For these, "level" only means *how far along the one ladder* — there is **no different milestone**, so forcing them into "levels" is exactly the error you spotted.

**vocabulary is the perfect example of ①** — and it has **two ladders at once** (just as you said): **breadth** (the *words* — Tier-1 everyday → Tier-2 sophisticated) and **depth** (the *use* — understands it → uses it on cue → uses it spontaneously in new talk). Different milestones on each; a child can be high on one and low on the other.
