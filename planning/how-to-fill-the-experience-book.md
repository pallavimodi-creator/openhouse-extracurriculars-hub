# how to fill the experience book — a educator guide
### part of the educator overview · read this once, then it's a 2-minute-a-day habit

You don't test children to fill this book. You **watch** them during class and record what they can already do. If you've never done this before, this page tells you exactly how. The book has two parts — the **daily page** and each child's **progress spread** — and you fill them at two different rhythms.

---

## the daily rhythm (under 2 minutes, right after class)

**fill the daily page** as soon as the session ends, while it's fresh:
1. tick the day's strand (language / art / stem).
2. tick the parts you ran + write the **one main thing** ("we read *The Gruffalo* and retold it with puppets").
3. write **one real moment** from today (one line) — something a *specific* child did. This goes home to parents as the day's update.

That's the whole daily page. Don't overthink it.

---

## marking skills — the only mechanic you need

You mark skills on each child's **progress spread**, mostly **during the closing circle** (that's your observe window — see "where to look" below). Here is the entire method:

> **when a child shows a rung *by themselves*, write today's date in that dot. Three dots filled = they've reached the ★ and can level up. If a child has been stuck on the same rung for weeks, draw a circle around it — that means "needs more help."**

Two words do all the work: **independently** (you didn't help them) and **more than once** (not a lucky one-off). If both are true → date the dot. If not → leave it; you'll catch it another day.

**you do NOT mark every child every day.** Pick **2–4 focus children** each session and watch them closely. Over a week, everyone gets seen. Trying to mark all 10 every day is what makes educators give up — don't.

**mark the skill, not the segment.** You never write down *which* part of the day it happened in. The programme overview already tells you where each skill shows up (below) — so you just look there, then tick the skill.

---

## where to look (so you observe the right skill in the right place)

Each segment grows one main skill — that's where you watch for it. (Full map is in the programme note for each strand.) For example, on a **language day**:

| watch for this skill… | …mainly here |
|---|---|
| storytelling & comprehension | book'o'clock + the closing retell |
| vocabulary | wordsmiths |
| pre-writing | play-writes |
| speaking & sequencing | the closing recap circle |
| listening / rhyme | circle-time song |

So in the closing recap ("what happened in our story today?") you can observe **speaking + storytelling** for your focus children in one go — that's the easiest place to mark.

---

## a worked example (a real language day)

> It's a language day. You read *The Gruffalo* and the children retold it with puppets. In the **closing recap circle** you ask, "what happened in the story?" Your 3 focus children today are Meera, Aarav and Kabir.
> - **Meera** retells it in order — the mouse, the woods, the gruffalo, how the mouse tricked everyone. That's *storytelling → going strong* (retells with story-bones), and she did it herself. → **date the dot.** ✓
> - **Aarav** names the puppets but can't put the events in order. He's at *getting there* (describes), not yet *going strong*. → leave the top dot blank; he's not there yet.
> - **Kabir** didn't say anything today. → **don't mark him** — you didn't see it, so you don't guess. Watch him tomorrow.
>
> You just recorded two children's storytelling from one closing circle, in under a minute.

## once a month — the "moment"

Write **one specific, real line per child per month** (aim for under 3 minutes a child):
> *"14 June — Aarav retold the goat story in order and added his own ending where they throw a party."*

The test: **if it could be said about any child, it's not specific enough.** "Doing well" fails. A real, observed moment passes. The artefact (their work or a photo) goes in the folder and travels home each term.

---

## the golden rules (when in doubt)

1. **observe, never test.** Never put a child on the spot to "check a box." If you didn't see it naturally, you don't mark it.
2. **independently + more than once** — or don't dot it.
3. **2–4 focus children a day**, not everyone.
4. **mark the skill, not the segment.**
5. **blank is better than wrong.** An empty dot just means "not seen yet," which is fine and honest.
6. **the circle is a kindness, not a failure** — it's how you and the parent know where to help next.

---

## common mistakes (don't do these)

- ❌ trying to mark all children every day → you'll burn out and stop. *Rotate focus children.*
- ❌ testing a child to fill a dot → that's not what this is. *Watch real class moments.*
- ❌ dotting a rung they did *with your help* → that's not "independent." *Wait for them to own it.*
- ❌ writing vague monthly moments ("good day") → *make it a specific, real thing.*
- ❌ recording the segment too → *skill only; the overview already maps the segments.*

> the point: the dots fill toward a star, the child sees their own journey, the parent sees real growth (not a grade), and you see — at a glance — who needs help with what. Keep it light; that's what makes it last all year.
