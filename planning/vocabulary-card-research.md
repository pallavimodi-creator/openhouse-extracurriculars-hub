# does this vocabulary approach work? — the evidence

**Short answer: yes — strongly.** The Wordsmiths flashcard design (book words · two tiers · flip card · the four-task game) lines up almost point-for-point with what the research says actually grows young children's vocabulary. Below is the assessment, pillar by pillar, with what to watch so it works in practice.

---

## the design, mapped to the evidence

### 1 · two decks (easy / hard) = tiered words
This is exactly **Beck, McKeown & Kucan's "robust vocabulary instruction."** They split words into **Tier 1** (common, everyday — *house, happy*) and **Tier 2** (sophisticated words a child meets in books but rarely in talk — *elegant, astounding, slumbering*). Their core finding: **directly teaching Tier-2 words is the high-leverage move** — children pick up Tier-1 words anyway, but Tier-2 words need deliberate instruction. Our **hard deck is the Tier-2 deck**, which is precisely where teaching effort pays off. They estimate ~400 words a year are worth teaching directly — a deck-a-week cadence fits that.

### 2 · the four tasks = the active, deep-processing core
The strongest part of the design. The research is blunt that **exposure alone is not enough** — children need *deep processing* and *active use across contexts*, not just to hear a definition. Each task is a known mechanism:

- **emote + enact** → **embodied / enactment learning.** Self-performed gestures and acting words out leave a *motor* trace in memory; studies find words are retained markedly better when children *imitate/perform* the action than when they only watch or listen — and the effect lasts months. Acting out a word is one of the most reliable boosts there is.
- **use it in a sentence** → **generative, active use.** Producing the word in your own sentence forces a richer representation than recognising a definition.
- **word conversation (what / why / where / how)** → **dialogic reading** (Whitehurst, Lonigan). Open-ended questioning during book talk is one of the best-evidenced early-vocabulary methods: children read to dialogically jump **months ahead** of children read to in the ordinary way, in just weeks.

Running all four on one word = **multiple, varied exposures with deepening processing** — exactly the recipe the depth-of-vocabulary research calls for.

### 3 · words pulled from the storybooks = words in meaningful context
The words aren't a decontextualised list — each is tied to a book the children have lived through, with the **in-story sentence** on the back. This anchors the word to a shared experience and a concrete event, which the research repeatedly flags as what makes a word *stick* (relate the word to children's experience, then stretch it to new contexts via the tasks).

### 4 · picture + word, and "pairs with" = dual coding + word networks
- **front: picture + word** → **dual coding** — pairing an image with the word gives two retrieval routes. (Gestures-plus-pictures studies show the biggest, most durable gains come from combining the visual with the active.)
- **back: meaning · type · "pairs with"** → builds the word into a **network** (collocations, related words), which is what *depth* of word knowledge means — not just "knows the definition" but "knows how the word behaves."

---

## what to watch (so it works in practice)

The research is equally clear about how these methods *fail* — keep these in view:

1. **Repetition matters — one pass won't do it.** Word learning is gradual and needs **multiple encounters over time.** Cycle the decks; bring a word back across days and in other segments (book'o'clock, closing recap), not just once.
2. **Depth beats the flip.** The card is the prompt; the **talk and the doing are the learning.** If it becomes "flip → read definition → next," the effect collapses. Protect time for the four tasks.
3. **Active turns, not just choral.** Embodiment and generation only count when the *child* performs/produces — give individual turns, not only whole-class call-and-response.
4. **Tier honestly.** The leverage is in the **hard (Tier-2) deck.** Make sure the easy/hard split is real (sophisticated book words in *hard*), and don't let "easy" Tier-1 words crowd out teaching time. *(Note: the current easy/hard split in the deck is a first pass by word difficulty — worth checking against your colour coding.)*
5. **Keep it short and playful for 3–5.** A few words, lots of acting and talk, stop while it's still fun.

---

## verdict
The approach is **well-founded** — it stacks four independently evidence-backed methods (tiered words · enactment · generative use · dialogic questioning) onto words children already met in story. That combination is close to a best-practice early-vocabulary design. The risk isn't the design; it's the *delivery* — keep the talk and the acting at the centre, repeat words over time, and protect the hard deck.
