# language through storytelling — programme & formats note
### the teacher source-of-truth · ages 3–5 · at-centre

> This is **Output ①** of the programme — the *goal · skills · learning outcomes · milestones · levels · format map*. Everything else derives from it: the **experience book** (the child's journey), the **activities** (how we teach each rung), and **teacher training**. Written for a brand-new teacher with little training — if it isn't here, don't assume it.

---

## 1 · the goal (and the one big why)

**parent-facing one line:**
> *"your child becomes a confident talker, a close listener, and a real storyteller — the foundation that all reading, writing and speaking is built on."*

**the one big why — school readiness.** This programme is not "early reading." It builds the thing school reading and writing actually stand on. A child is *ready for school* when they have:

1. **strong oral language** — a big spoken vocabulary, the ability to follow and tell a story, to listen and to answer — because **you cannot understand a sentence you read if you can't understand it when you hear it.**
2. **the learning habits of a school child** — sitting in a circle, taking turns, focusing on a task, regulating big feelings, trying again. (NCF Foundational Stage names these *positive learning habits* as a curricular goal in their own right.)

So "how do children become ready for school here?" — **not** by learning letters early, but by becoming children who *understand language deeply and behave like learners*. When formal reading starts at school, these children read **with meaning** from day one, because the hard, slow-to-build part — language and comprehension — is already strong.

---

## 2 · why we don't teach the letters yet — and why that's not only okay, it's the point

Parents will ask *"but are they reading? are they learning letters?"* The honest, confident answer:

**Reading is two prongs, not one.** Reading science (e.g. Ohio State University's framing) describes a **two-pronged approach**: children must learn to **decode** words (sound out letters → words) *and*, at the same time, build the **oral language and comprehension** that lets them *understand* what they decode. A child who can decode but can't comprehend is "barking at print" — saying words without meaning.

> *"Reading science dictates a two-pronged approach to teaching children to read — helping them learn to decode and read words, while simultaneously bolstering oral language and other skills that allow them to comprehend what they read."*

**At 3–5 we deliberately build the comprehension prong — oral language — because:**
- it is the **harder, slower prong to build**, and the one most predictive of later reading *and* writing success. Decoding is more mechanical and can be taught systematically and quickly when a child is ready.
- a strong oral foundation makes decoding *land*: when letters arrive (formal school / our 5+ pathway), these children attach sound-symbols to a rich language they already own, and **comprehend immediately**.
- pushing letters/phonics too early, without the oral base, produces decoders who don't understand — the classic mistake.

**so what we DO build (the oral prong):** listening, speaking, vocabulary, storytelling and narrative, and the *oral* sound-play that bridges to phonics later — **rhyme, syllable-clapping, and hearing first/last sounds** (phonological awareness). This is sound *play*, not letter *drill*. We hold formal grapheme/letter teaching for when it belongs.

> one-line for the front desk: *"we're not skipping reading — we're building the half of reading that's hardest to build and matters most: understanding language. letters come easily on top of it."*

## 3 · the skills (the spine) — the oral track of the spiral

Five skills, each with an **ability ladder → ★ north star**. (Names are stable across the band; they deepen by level.) Drawn from your spiral's oral track (Listening · Speaking · Reading-as-comprehension · pre-Writing) — **letters/decoding are intentionally absent at this stage.**

| skill | what it really is | ability ladder → ★ |
|---|---|---|
| **listening** | attend to talk and stories; hear sounds in words | attend → recall → follow → **discriminate sounds (rhyme/first-sound) ★** |
| **speaking** | say words → sentences → tell things | name → phrase → sentence → **narrate ★** |
| **storytelling & comprehension** | make meaning of a story; retell and reshape it | point/name → describe → answer who/where/why → **retell with story-bones ★** |
| **vocabulary** | own and use new words | meet a word → understand it → **use it in talk ★** |
| **pre-writing (mark-making)** | hand control for writing readiness — *not* letters | scribble → pattern → controlled stroke → **forms shapes/own name shape ★** |

> note on pre-writing: this is **fine-motor + left-to-right + grip** (your spiral's "left-to-right progression practice patterns — lllll, ////, ∿∿∿"), i.e. *getting the hand ready to write*, distinct from teaching letters to read. A child can be going-strong here and still not be taught grapheme decoding — by design.

## 4 · levels — same activity, every child at their own rung

The programme runs in **three levels within 3–5** (roughly: **L1** newest/youngest · **L2** developing · **L3** ready-for-5+). Crucially, **level is per-skill, not per-child** — a child can be L3 in storytelling and L1 in pre-writing — and **the same activity is run at every level at once** by changing the rung, not the activity. (Exactly your number example: in one number game, a 3-year-old counts to 5 while a 5-year-old does take-away — same game, different rung.)

**the language LO ladders (× level)** — each cell is what "going strong" looks like at that level; the rung *below* is "getting there", and just meeting it is "starting out". Every LO carries a **check** (👁 = observe it; the teacher watches for it — never a test).

| skill | level 1 (going strong) | level 2 (going strong) | level 3 ★ (going strong) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **listening** | sits for a short story & shows they're following (👁 looks, reacts) | recalls 2–3 things that happened (👁 names events) | hears & plays with sounds — rhymes, claps syllables, says the first sound of a word (👁 produces a rhyme / first sound) |
| **speaking** | answers with a word or two (👁 responds on cue) | speaks in a full sentence; answers *who / where* (👁 a sentence unprompted) | narrates an event or retells in sequence (👁 a 3-part telling) |
| **storytelling & comprehension** | points to & names what's in the picture (👁 names 3 things) | describes what's happening; answers *why* (👁 a "because…") | retells with the **story-bones** — character · setting · problem · attempt · ending; changes the ending (👁 includes ≥3 bones) |
| **vocabulary** | repeats & acts a new word (see→act→say) (👁 acts it) | shows they understand it (points/chooses correctly) (👁 correct choice) | uses the new word in their own talk that day (👁 spontaneous use) |
| **pre-writing** | makes marks & scribbles with control (👁 holds & marks) | copies patterns left-to-right (lllll, ////, ∿) (👁 a pattern strip) | forms simple shapes / the shape of their own name (👁 a recognisable form) |

**★ north star (language, end of 3–5):** *listens to a story, retells it in their own words with the story-bones in order, uses new words, and speaks confidently to the group — ready to attach letters to a language they already own.*

**promotion = attainment, not birthday.** A child moves up a level in a skill when they hit its ★ rung **independently, more than once.** Nothing else gates it.

## 5 · which segment grows which skill — *so you know where to look*

Each segment has a **primary skill** it grows and observes best. Teach to it, and observe *that* skill there — don't try to watch everything everywhere.

| segment (language day) | primary skill(s) | what to watch for |
|---|---|---|
| circle — song (roll & rhyme) | **listening** · vocabulary (rhyme/sound) | joins in, anticipates words, hears rhyme |
| circle — greeting / calendar / intention | **speaking** · learning habits | answers, names the day, holds the intention |
| wordsmiths | **vocabulary** · speaking | acts out & uses the new word |
| book'o'clock (deepened read-aloud) | **storytelling & comprehension** · listening · vocabulary | follows, answers *why*, retells with story-bones |
| play-writes | **pre-writing** | grip, controlled marks, patterns left-to-right |
| playground (game) | speaking · listening · vocabulary *(varies by game)* | plays, talks, uses words |
| closing reflection | **speaking** · storytelling (recap = sequence) · learning habits | recaps in order, speaks to the group |

> reading: book'o'clock is the storytelling/comprehension window; play-writes is the only pre-writing window; wordsmiths owns vocabulary. The closing recap is the best, lowest-effort place to *observe* speaking & sequencing for the experience book.

## 6 · how this feeds the rest (so points 5 & 6 derive from here)

- **the experience book (next deliverable)** tracks each child's **rung per skill** from this table and their journey toward the ★ — that *is* the milestone journey. The book's "how did each child do" marks map 1:1 to these LOs and their 👁 checks. A child stuck at L1 listening for weeks = "needs more help here," visible to teacher and parent.
- **the activities (next deliverable)** are *how we teach each rung*. Each activity will name **which skill + which rung** it works, and come as a **fixed set of formats** (e.g. 5 opening songs, named, rotating all year) so a new teacher knows exactly what to run, with built-in repetition for mastery and variety for interest. (See the format-set principle below.)

### the format-set principle (answering point 6)
For every recurring slot we define a **small fixed set of formats** (typically 4–6), each:
- **named** and **scripted** (exact words, materials, steps) — teacher-proof;
- tied to a **skill/rung** it serves (so variety isn't random — each format gets at a slightly different sub-skill);
- **rotated on a known schedule** so the child gets *repetition* (mastery) and the teacher gets *variety* (not bored, never guessing).

Worked example — **opening songs & movement (5, fixed for the year):**
1. *good morning song* (greeting · names) — Mon
2. *if you're happy / feelings song* (emotion words) — Tue
3. *head-shoulders-knees-toes* (body vocabulary · listening) — Wed
4. *the wheels on the bus / actions song* (sequence · actions) — Thu
5. *a rhyming song of the month* (rhyme awareness) — Fri
> same 5 all year → the child masters and *anticipates* them (which builds the security 3–5s need), the teacher never has to invent, and across the week all five touch different language sub-skills. The *exact* song list + lyrics + actions ship in the activity pack.
